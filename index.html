What is Naegleria fowleri?

The term "brain-eating amoeba" refers to an infamous microorganism scientifically known as Naegleria fowleri. This single-celled organism resides in warm freshwater environments, such as lakes, hot springs, and poorly maintained swimming pools. Though it may conjure up nightmarish images, Naegleria fowleri does not cause harm under normal circumstances. It is only when water containing the amoeba enters the body through the nose—often during activities like swimming or diving—that danger arises.

History of Naegleria fowleri

The "brain-eating amoeba" is a colloquial term for Naegleria fowleri, one of the microscopic amoebas that can cause a rare but deadly brain infection known as primary amoebic meningoencephalitis, PAM. Naegleria fowleri was first found in the 1960s when cases of brain infections were found in Australia. This organism usually is a single-celled creature living in warm freshwater bodies like lakes, hot springs, and poorly chlorinated swimming pools. Naegleria fowleri penetrate the human body via the nose. Most people get infested with this amoeba while swimming or diving in warm freshwater. The amoeba moves up the olfactory nerve upon entry into the nasal cavity and eventually reaches the brain, where it begins destroying the tissue of the brain. This progresses the illness known as PAM, which is rapidly fatal. Other countries have reported it, but the infections are scarce and scattered throughout, and it is comparatively rare in the United States to have more than a few cases reported in any given year, mainly from warmer southern states. Public health measures have generally focused on prevention of warm, stagnant waters during hot weather, especially during warmer times in areas where Naegleria fowleri has been prevalent. Though rare, infection cases entail very high case fatality and limited effective treatment, so the important issues are education and prevention.

While the notion of a "brain-eating" organism is terrifying, it's essential to remember that occurrences of PAM are rare. The parasite thrives in specific environments and typically affects young, healthy individuals carried into warm water adventures. Awareness, education, and safe swimming practices can significantly reduce the risks associated with this formidable foe, ensuring that our encounters with nature remain thrilling rather than threatening.

How do we get infected by Naegleria fowleri?

Infection by the "brain-eating amoeba," or Naegleria fowleri, occurs when water containing the amoeba enters the body through the nose. Here's a breakdown of how the infection happens:
Exposure to Contaminated Water: Naegleria fowleri lives in warm freshwater environments, like lakes, rivers, hot springs, and poorly maintained or unchlorinated swimming pools. People typically encounter the amoeba while swimming, diving, or engaging in water sports in these types of water bodies.
Entry Through the Nose: Infection only occurs when contaminated water is under pressure and enters through the nasal cavities. It is stated to occur when someone dives, jumps, or falls into water in a manner that forces water into the nose. The amoeba cannot infect via contaminated water drinking; it has to enter the nasal opening.
Migration to the Brain: Once it has entered the nasal cavity, Naegleria fowleri enters the olfactory nerve that transmits the sense of smell and eventually into the brain.
Destruction of Brain Tissue: If it moves into the brain, the amoeba will start destroying the brain tissue resulting in the primary amoebic meningoencephalitis (PAM), which is an inflammation destruction of the brain tissue.

What are the symptoms of infection with Naegleria fowleri?

Primary amoebic meningoencephalitis (PAM) is the illness caused by the brain-eating amoeba, Naegleria fowleri. Symptoms start between 1 to 12 days following infection from contact with contaminated water and then rapidly worsen over several days. This is a general outline of how symptoms develop:
Early Symptoms (within 1–3 days after infection)
Early symptoms are similar to bacterial meningitis. These may include:
Serious and abrupt headache
Fever
Nausea and vomiting
Late Symptoms (as the infection worsens)
As the Naegleria fowleri infection destroys brain tissue, there is the emergence of neurological symptoms, which typically come rapidly:
Stiff neck
Sensitive to light
Disoriented and confused
Loses balance or coordination
Seizures
Hallucinations
Coma
Disease Progression and Lethality
When the symptoms appear, infection progresses rapidly and is usually fatal, killing in 3 to 7 days. Almost everybody infected with PAM succumbs to death, and survival rate is estimated to be less than 3%. Of course, certain patients have survived once receiving aggressive medical care.

PREVENTIONS

Preventing infection with Naegleria fowleri requires keeping exposure to contaminated warm freshwater limited. Here are some of the key prevention tips:
1. Avoid Warm Freshwater Exposure:
Limit Activities in Warm Fresh Water: Avoid swimming, diving, or engaging in other water sports in warm fresh water bodies, such as lakes and rivers, and hot springs, especially during summer and also in regions where Naegleria fowleri has been detected. Be wary of shallow, stagnant bodies of water, as Naegleria fowleri thrives there more than in any other kind of water.
2. Protect your nose:
Use Nose Clips: If you swim or dive in warm freshwater, wearing nose clips can prevent water from entering your nasal passages, where the amoeba can gain access to the brain. Avoid forcing water up your nose: Do not dive or jump into the water or splash forceful water into the nostril. Always keep your head out of the water, especially during fresh water lakes and rivers.
3. Never use untreated or unsterile water to nasal irrigate
Always Use Sterile Water: Sterile water or distillated water is preferred for practices such as use of a neti pot or other nasal rinse devices. Instead, tap water has been known to contain low levels of Naegleria fowleri at times, avoid its usage for such purposes. Clean the Nasal Devices Appropriately: Clean and dry your nasal rinsing devices appropriately after every use.
4. Pool and Hot Tub Hygiene.
Maintenance of Pools with Right Chlorination: Ensure chlorinated pools, hot tubs, and other recreational water outlets have chlorine at the right concentration, keeping these away from contamination. Naegleria fowleri cannot survive in well chlorinated water. Check Water Temperature and Maintenance Level. The risk increases with rising temperature and poor maintenance. It would require one to keep water below 104°F (40°C). Checking chlorine levels regularly.
5. Awareness of Public Health Advisories
Stay Informed: Be aware of any advisories regarding Naegleria fowleri in local water sources, especially during summer. Follow Guidance on Water Activities: Refrain from high-risk activities in natural freshwater environments if there is an advisory in your area.
